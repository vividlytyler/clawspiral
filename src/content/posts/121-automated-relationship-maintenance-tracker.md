You don't stop caring about people. You just get busy, and then a month passes, then three, and suddenly you realize you haven't talked to your college roommate in six months. The friendship didn't end — it just quietly faded because no one made the first move.

This is the problem an OpenClaw relationship tracker solves. Not by being a CRM with deal stages and follow-up reminders, but by being the friend who says "hey, you haven't checked in with Sam in a while."

## How It Works

The system is built around a simple file: a list of the people who matter to you, grouped by relationship type, with notes on what you last talked about and when.

```
## Close Friends
- Jordan | Last contact: June 3 | Notes: Just had a baby, starting to sleep through nights
- Sam | Last contact: May 15 | Notes: Went through the job change, feeling better now
- Alex | Last contact: July 1 | Notes: Planning the Portland trip for September

## Family
- Mom | Last contact: July 10 | Notes: Her knee is better, gardening again
- Dad | Last contact: June 28 | Notes: Retired last month, getting bored
- Cousin Riley | Last contact: April 3 | Notes: Engaged! Wedding in October

## Work Friends
- Priya | Last contact: May 22 | Notes: Left the company, freelancing now
- Chen | Last contact: June 14 | Notes: Moved to the new office downtown
```

When you have a good conversation with someone, you log it in passing:

> "Caught up with Jordan today, she's doing great with the baby."

OpenClaw updates the last contact date and adds your note. No forms, no app, just a message.

## The "Haven't Heard From You" Reminder

This is where it earns its place. OpenClaw runs a regular check — weekly works well — and scans your list for anyone who's gone quiet.

For someone you've marked as a close friend, radio silence beyond 3 weeks triggers a gentle nudge:

> "It's been 19 days since you last checked in with Sam. You noted things were tense at their job last time — might be worth reaching out."

For family, the threshold is shorter — 10 days is a good default. For the wider network of acquaintances you want to keep in orbit, 6 weeks is reasonable.

The reminder includes context: what you last talked about, any notes you've made, and a suggested opening if you want one. It doesn't send the message for you — it just makes sure the intention doesn't get lost in the noise of daily life.

## Birthday and Occasion Tracking

Alongside the contact log, you can store important dates: birthdays, anniversaries, the big things you want to remember. OpenClaw can check these daily and remind you in the morning:

> "Riley's birthday is in 5 days. You noted she's planning an October wedding — might be a good time to check in about that."

For family and close friends, this means you're never caught off guard by a "happy birthday" text that arrives three days late. For people like old colleagues you only see on occasions, it's the difference between remembering and realizing six months too late that you missed their big moment.

## What to Say — Drafted for You

One of the friction points in reconnecting is the blank page: what do you even say to someone you haven't talked to in two months? OpenClaw can draft something for you based on your notes:

> "Draft a message to Sam. Last we talked they were stressed about the job situation — they're in a better place now based on what I noted. Keep it warm and short, acknowledge it's been a while."

OpenClaw generates a draft. You edit it, send it from your own phone, and the conversation continues. The system doesn't replace your voice — it just removes the "I don't know what to say" paralysis that lets silence stretch into months.

## Setting It Up

**The relationship file** lives in your OpenClaw workspace. You can seed it from your contacts or just build it over time — OpenClaw can even help you populate it by scanning your recent messages and asking "who are the 10 people you most want to stay in touch with?"

**Grouping** is up to you. Close friends, family, work network, old college friends — whatever makes sense for how you think about your relationships. Groups can have different silence thresholds.

**The check interval** is a weekly cron job that scans for overdue contacts and sends you a digest. Daily reminders for birthdays and upcoming occasions keep the important dates visible.

**Notes** are the most important part. They don't need to be elaborate — "going through a hard time," "just had surgery," "planning a trip" is enough. The notes make the reminders useful instead of generic.

## What It Won't Do

This won't manage your calendar or automatically schedule coffee dates. It won't integrate with your social media to detect when someone posted something new. It won't send messages for you or pretend to be you.

What it will do is make sure the relationships you care about don't silently atrophy because life got in the way and nobody remembered to call.

The gap between "meaning to reach out" and "actually reaching out" is usually about 30 seconds of friction. OpenClaw removes that friction — not by automating the connection, but by making sure you don't forget it was supposed to happen.
