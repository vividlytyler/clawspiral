Six months ago, OpenClaw was a weekend project born out of frustration — a single claw and a Discord server at creator Peter Steinberger's place in Austria. Today, it is a global movement generating 4.5 million new installations per week and holding the title of fastest-growing repository in GitHub history. And now, a foundation.

On July 8, 2026, the OpenClaw Foundation officially launched as a US 501(c)(3) non-profit organization. The structure is deliberate: the foundation exists to protect OpenClaw's independence and ensure it remains MIT-licensed, open, and community-driven for the long term.

"Our vision is to bring people closer to AI — in service of human potential, not in place of it," the foundation stated in its founding announcement. "Today AI feels distant and a little frightening, locked in someone else's cloud, answering to someone else's interests. We think the future is personal AI that actually does things. It runs on your machine, works with the apps that you already use, and answers to you and you alone."

**"Your agent, your machine, your rules."**

Steinberger, who joined OpenAI earlier this year, will continue making technical decisions for the project. OpenAI has made a "commitment" to keep OpenClaw open and independent, though the specifics of that commitment have not been publicly detailed.

The foundation's stated ambition is to make OpenClaw "the Switzerland of AI" — neutral ground where every model provider and lab can plug into the technology and collaborate on agent standards. That work is reportedly already underway through foundation-convened working groups.

The move places OpenClaw alongside landmark open-source projects like Linux, Apache, and Mozilla, which similarly institutionalized governance through neutral stewards before becoming critical infrastructure. Whether a small team running a rapidly scaling open-source project can maintain that neutrality while one of its early backers is a major AI laboratory remains an open question the community will be watching closely.
