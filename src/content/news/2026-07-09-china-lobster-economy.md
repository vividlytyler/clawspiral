In Shenzhen, "have you raised a lobster yet?" has become the tech industry's favorite greeting. The nickname — 龙虾, a nod to OpenClaw's logo — reflects a remarkable grassroots adoption wave that has turned the AI agent into China's latest consumer obsession.

Feng Qingyang started offering OpenClaw installation support on Xianyu (Alibaba's secondhand marketplace) in January. By February he'd quit his day job. His operation now employs over 100 people, has processed 7,000 orders at roughly 248 RMB (~$34) each, and generated nearly $240,000 in revenue — from typing commands into a terminal on someone else's behalf.

## An economy built on friction

The gap is simple: everyone wants OpenClaw, but setup requires terminal commands, developer platform navigation, and compatible hardware. On Taobao and JD.com, hundreds of listings offer installation guides and support packages from 100–700 RMB ($15–$100). At the high end, technicians show up in person.

Li Gong, a Shenzhen refurbished Mac seller, started shipping machines with OpenClaw preinstalled. Orders have increased eightfold in two weeks.

## Mass adoption, government attention

This isn't just freelancers. Tencent held a public installation event where the line included elderly users and children. The Longgang district government in Shenzhen released policies supporting OpenClaw ventures with free computing credits and cash subsidies; Wuxi followed.

Meetups draw 500+ people. The largest, on March 7, packed over 1,000 attendees.

## The security pushback

Not everyone is buying in. China's cybersecurity regulator CNCERT issued a warning about data breach risks tied to OpenClaw adoption. Critics argue the agent is still a proof of concept and that most new users lack the technical judgment to use it safely.

"Have you raised a lobster yet?" — the question has gone mainstream. Whether that's a feature or a bug is the trillion-dollar question.
